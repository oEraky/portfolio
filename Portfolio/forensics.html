Evaluating Forensics Professors' Needs with the ForensicsXR Teacher's App


  • Role: UI/UX + Web Developer
  • Tools: Unity, JavaScript, HTML, CSS
  • Team: 1 design researcher, 2 developers, 1 forensic expert

The Problem


• Teachers need a way to evaluate students.
• Teachers need to see the work their students are doing in a way that makes sense. Math is great, but how do we translate data points into real items?
• Teachers need to be able to generate their own crime scenes for students to explore.


Technical Implementation


I used my technical knowledge of Javascript, HTML and CSS here, connecting my background as a developer with my design oriented mind.

• Use user cards on the homepage to give an easy idea of who each student is, with user IDs for privacy.
• Allow the instructor to see the images their students took, with the date and time the images were taken; that way the professor can see the story their students are telling.
• Create a heatmap that showed the students time spent in each VR point with intensifiying colors, to show consistent movement through the scene!
• Make a function for professors to generate their own crime scenes with simple, drag and drop functionality. All of the functionality, without them needing to worry about too much technical understanding.



User Research


Aside from developing the systems mentioned above, I also played a significant role in user research and understanding.

• I led the post experiment survey sessions in a class of 60 students to enhance the VR application.
• I ran one on one interviews with each student who participated to evaluate their needs with the VR application.
• I used qualitative coding to analyze the three major pain points of the VR App: navigation, controller feedback, and visual design.



Our Outcomes


The VR application's supporter rate increased by 30% after our subsequent experiments.
• The Teacher's app showed an increase in instructor understanding of student activities.
• I made further developments that halved the time it took to upload student information. Before data entry was manual, so it took about 15 minutes to upload data, with the changes I made, that was shortened to just 1 minute.
• We were able to secure more funding because of the findings I made!



What I Learned


• Do more research. There were many components I did not understand about the project that I should have researched prior.
• Be timely. To keep up with the intensive workload without burning out, I learned to be more timely.
• Be confident. This project left little room for anxiety, the best work comes when you believe you are good enough to do it.
